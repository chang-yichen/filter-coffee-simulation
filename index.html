The Equations, Explained Simply

1. Darcy's Law — How Water Flows Through Coffee

The fundamental equation of percolation brewing
Darcy's Law Q = (k · A) / (μ · L) · (ρ · g · h)

This predicts the drip rate Q — how fast coffee flows out the bottom:

  • k (permeability) — How easily water passes through the coffee bed. Coarser grinds = bigger gaps = higher k = faster flow.
  • A (area) — Cross-sectional area of the dripper.
  • μ (viscosity) — How "thick" the water is. Hotter water is less viscous, so it flows faster.
  • L (bed depth) — Deeper bed = slower flow. This is why coffee dose matters!
  • ρ·g·h (pressure) — Weight of the water column. Taller column = more pressure = faster flow.
The coffee bed is like a packed sponge. Coarse grounds are a loose sponge (water runs through); fine grounds are dense (water squeezes through tiny gaps). Hot water is thin like oil — slides through easily — while cold water is thicker, like honey.

2. Brew Time — How Long Until the Last Drop

Derived from Darcy's Law for a cylindrical dripper
Total Brew Time T = (ν · L) / (k · g) · ln(R · ρc / ρw)
  • ν — Kinematic viscosity. Hotter → lower ν → faster brew.
  • L — Bed depth. Deeper = longer brew.
  • k — Permeability. Coarser grind = shorter brew.
  • R — Brew ratio (water/coffee). Inside a logarithm, so effect is gentle.

Key insight: dripper width doesn't appear! Only bed depth controls time.

Draining a bathtub through gravel: making the tub wider doesn't change drain time. More area means faster drainage but also more water — they cancel out. Only gravel thickness matters.

3. Noyes-Whitney — How Coffee Dissolves

The rate at which flavor compounds leave the grounds
Dissolution Rate dm/dt = A · (D / L) · (Csat − Csol)
  • A (surface area) — Finer grind = massively more surface = faster extraction.
  • D (diffusion coefficient) — Higher temp → higher D → faster extraction.
  • Csat − Csol — Extraction slows as water gets concentrated. Fresh water extracts fastest.
Dissolving sugar: powdered sugar (fine grind) vanishes fast; a sugar cube (coarse) is slow. Stirring helps — just as flowing water keeps "refreshing" the grounds with clean water.

4. Einstein-Smoluchowski — Temperature & Diffusion

Why hotter water extracts more and different flavors
Diffusion Coefficient D = μp · kB · T

Temperature has a double effect: (1) dissolves compounds faster at the surface, and (2) transports them away faster via diffusion. Different molecules have different mobilities — that's why temperature changes the flavor profile, not just speed.

Molecules in water are like people in a crowd. Cold = shuffling slowly. Hot = moving fast, bumping into each other — everything spreads and mixes quickly.

5. Extraction Yield — Measuring Your Brew

The fraction of coffee that ended up in your cup
Extraction Yield EY = (C · B) / D

C = TDS concentration, B = beverage weight, D = coffee dose.

Ideal: 18–22%. Below 18% = sour/grassy (under). Above 22% = bitter/astringent (over). Only ~30% of coffee is soluble — the art is extracting just the right amount.

Starting with 20g of coffee and extracting 4g of solubles = 20% yield. You can't get everything out, and you wouldn't want to — the last compounds taste harsh.

6. Kinematic Viscosity — Temperature & Flow

Why hotter water flows faster through the bed
Kinematic Viscosity ν = μ / ρ

As temperature rises, viscosity drops significantly. Flow speeds up ~20% from 80°C to 95°C. If you brew cooler, grind coarser to compensate.

Cold honey pours slowly; warm honey pours easily. Water does the same — less dramatically, but at the scale of tiny gaps between coffee grounds, it matters a lot.
